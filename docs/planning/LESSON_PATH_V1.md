# Smalltalk Coach — V1 Lesson Path

## 1. Purpose and status

**Purpose:** This document locks the v1 beginner learning path: the twelve
sequential lessons that turn a newcomer’s everyday office or campus moments
into teachable, reusable conversation skills. It is a product-design decision,
not a technical content schema or implementation specification.

**Date:** 2026-07-18

**Status:** Locked for v1 planning. This path was defined by design judgment
under the founder’s interview-gate waiver, not by completed user interviews.
It follows `docs/planning/DECISIONS.md` → **“2026-07-18 — Validation
Interviews Deferred; Build Gate Waived (Founder)”** and root `DECISIONS.md` →
**“2026-07-18 — Build Scope Gate Narrowed (Interview Thresholds Waived)”**.
Interview-dependent positions are explicitly marked as assumptions in §6.

The path is written for **Relocated Newcomers**: early-career professionals
and students who moved for work or school within roughly the last six months.
Its office/campus setting is a focused content world, while the product’s
positioning remains welcoming to all newcomers.

## 2. Path structure

The beginner path uses four sequential units and twelve lessons. It follows
the intended skill progression: start a conversation, sustain it, deepen it,
then end it in a way that makes future contact possible. Each lesson teaches
one behavior, so a user practices judgment rather than memorizing a script.

| Unit | Lessons | Skill movement | Newcomer setting used throughout |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Get Started | L01–L03 | Notice an opening and begin naturally | First week at a new office; campus orientation and coffee queues |
| 2. Keep It Moving | L04–L06 | Exchange turns, listen, and ask useful follow-ups | Cafeteria tables, shared desks, and the walk to a first meeting |
| 3. Make It Mutual | L07–L09 | Find common ground while respecting engagement and boundaries | A class break, building lobby, team lunch, and after-class social |
| 4. Leave a Door Open | L10–L12 | Deepen selectively, exit warmly, and create continuity | After-work gathering, club event, study session, and meeting neighbors |

**Sequence rule:** Unit order is fixed in v1. A user may revisit any completed
lesson, but the next new lesson is introduced in sequence so the practice
builds from opening to continuity rather than presenting disconnected tips.

## 3. The locked v1 lesson list

### Unit 1 — Get Started
#### L01 — First hello

- **id:** `l01-first-hello`
- **title:** First hello
- **concept:** Open with a simple greeting plus a small, shared-context cue
  instead of searching for an impressive line.
- **skill objective:** User can start a brief conversation with a new
  colleague or classmate using an observable cue from the moment.
- **example situation:** On their first morning at a new office, the user is
  waiting beside a colleague for coffee before the team stand-up.
- **practice scenario type:** Short roleplay: greet a colleague at the coffee
  machine and choose a context cue to add.
- **completion check type:** Draft-and-explain check: write one opening and
  identify the shared-context cue that makes it appropriate.
- **dimension mapping:** Warmth, flow.

#### L02 — Use what is around you

- **id:** `l02-use-the-setting`
- **title:** Use the setting
- **concept:** Turn a neutral detail in the environment into a low-pressure
  conversation opening.
- **skill objective:** User can spot a relevant, non-invasive conversation
  opener in a shared office or campus setting.
- **example situation:** At campus orientation, the user notices several
  people comparing maps while waiting for the next session to begin.
- **practice scenario type:** Choose-the-better-reply: select the opener that
  fits a waiting-room moment without making assumptions about the other person.
- **completion check type:** Situation-match check: match three environment
  cues to openings and explain why one cue is too personal.
- **dimension mapping:** Warmth, flow.

#### L03 — Ask an easy first question

- **id:** `l03-easy-first-question`
- **title:** Ask an easy first question
- **concept:** Use one open, answerable question that gives the other person
  room to choose what to share.
- **skill objective:** User can turn a context cue into one natural first
  question without interrogating the other person.
- **example situation:** Before a first project meeting, the user and a new
  teammate are waiting for the video room to become available.
- **practice scenario type:** Draft-then-compare: turn three context cues into
  questions and compare their likely pressure level.
- **completion check type:** Revise-and-reason check: improve a closed or
  over-personal question and state what makes the revision easier to answer.
- **dimension mapping:** Curiosity, flow.

### Unit 2 — Keep It Moving
#### L04 — Answer, then pass it back

- **id:** `l04-answer-and-return`
- **title:** Answer, then return
- **concept:** Give a brief answer with one useful detail, then return a
  related question so the exchange has two active participants.
- **skill objective:** User can respond to a casual question without either
  stopping at one word or taking over the conversation.
- **example situation:** A colleague at a cafeteria table asks how the user is
  finding the city during their first week.
- **practice scenario type:** Short roleplay: answer a colleague’s question,
  add one detail, and return a related question.
- **completion check type:** Turn-balance check: choose the response that
  shares enough and invites the other person back in, then explain the choice.
- **dimension mapping:** Reciprocity, curiosity.

#### L05 — Show you heard them

- **id:** `l05-show-you-heard`
- **title:** Show you heard them
- **concept:** Acknowledge one specific detail before moving to the next
  question or topic.
- **skill objective:** User can make a conversation partner feel heard by
  responding to what they actually said rather than pivoting immediately.
- **example situation:** During a campus welcome lunch, a classmate mentions
  that they are still figuring out the bus routes.
- **practice scenario type:** Choose-the-better-reply: distinguish a generic
  acknowledgement, an attentive response, and an abrupt topic change.
- **completion check type:** Evidence-spotting check: highlight the detail
  being acknowledged and select the response that follows it naturally.
- **dimension mapping:** Warmth, reciprocity.

#### L06 — Follow the thread

- **id:** `l06-follow-the-thread`
- **title:** Follow the thread
- **concept:** Ask one follow-up that grows from the other person’s last
  detail instead of switching to a prepared list of questions.
- **skill objective:** User can form one relevant follow-up from a partner’s
  answer in a casual office or campus conversation.
- **example situation:** A teammate says they joined a local running group
  after moving; the conversation happens while walking back from lunch.
- **practice scenario type:** Short roleplay: choose and ask one follow-up
  after a teammate shares an interest.
- **completion check type:** Branching-response check: select the follow-up
  that follows the stated detail and reject an unrelated interview-style one.
- **dimension mapping:** Curiosity, reciprocity.

### Unit 3 — Make It Mutual
#### L07 — Share, then make space

- **id:** `l07-share-and-make-space`
- **title:** Share, then make space
- **concept:** Offer a small, relevant personal detail and leave room for the
  other person to respond or take the topic elsewhere.
- **skill objective:** User can contribute to common ground without turning a
  conversation into a monologue.
- **example situation:** At a new-neighbor building event, the user discovers
  another resident also arrived recently and mentions one adjustment they have
  enjoyed.
- **practice scenario type:** Draft-then-compare: add one detail to a reply,
  then compare versions that overshare, undershare, or invite reciprocity.
- **completion check type:** Balance check: revise an overly long self-share
  into a concise contribution with space for the other person.
- **dimension mapping:** Reciprocity, curiosity.

#### L08 — Let a pause be normal

- **id:** `l08-handle-the-pause`
- **title:** Let a pause be normal
- **concept:** Treat a short pause as a normal moment to notice, extend, or
  close—not proof that the interaction has failed.
- **skill objective:** User can choose a low-pressure next move after a brief
  pause rather than rushing into unrelated talk.
- **example situation:** During a break after a campus workshop, a chat with a
  classmate pauses while both look toward the refreshment table.
- **practice scenario type:** Choose-the-next-move: decide whether to extend,
  use the setting, or end kindly after a pause.
- **completion check type:** Scenario-judgment check: select a proportionate
  response to three different pauses and explain the cue behind the choice.
- **dimension mapping:** Flow, warmth.

#### L09 — Notice interest and boundaries

- **id:** `l09-read-the-room`
- **title:** Read the room
- **concept:** Use observable signs of engagement or disengagement to decide
  whether to continue, lighten the interaction, or give the person space.
- **skill objective:** User can name a visible cue and choose a respectful
  next step without claiming to know another person’s intent.
- **example situation:** At an after-class social, a classmate answers briefly
  while repeatedly looking toward friends who have just arrived.
- **practice scenario type:** Cue-sort exercise: sort observable cues into
  “continue gently,” “check once,” and “give space.”
- **completion check type:** Evidence-and-boundary check: identify the cue in
  a short exchange and choose the least pressuring response.
- **dimension mapping:** Curiosity, flow.

### Unit 4 — Leave a Door Open
#### L10 — Build on a shared topic

- **id:** `l10-build-on-common-ground`
- **title:** Build on common ground
- **concept:** Deepen a conversation by staying with one genuinely shared
  topic long enough to explore it together.
- **skill objective:** User can turn a discovered shared interest into one
  specific, mutual conversational thread.
- **example situation:** At an office after-work gathering, the user and a
  colleague discover they are both looking for inexpensive weekend activities
  in the new city.
- **practice scenario type:** Short roleplay: develop a shared-topic opening
  for two turns without jumping to unrelated questions.
- **completion check type:** Topic-thread check: arrange mixed conversation
  turns into a coherent shared-topic sequence and explain the connection.
- **dimension mapping:** Curiosity, reciprocity.

#### L11 — End warmly

- **id:** `l11-end-warmly`
- **title:** End warmly
- **concept:** End a conversation with a clear, friendly close that respects
  time and avoids an abrupt disappearance.
- **skill objective:** User can leave a casual office or campus conversation
  with a warm close and a context-appropriate reason to go.
- **example situation:** After chatting with a colleague in the coffee corner,
  the user needs to join a scheduled onboarding meeting.
- **practice scenario type:** Draft-then-compare: choose a warm exit that is
  neither overexplained nor dismissive.
- **completion check type:** Exit-quality check: revise an abrupt close into
  one that signals appreciation, a reason, and a clean ending.
- **dimension mapping:** Warmth, flow.

#### L12 — Make continuity easy

- **id:** `l12-make-continuity-easy`
- **title:** Make continuity easy
- **concept:** When mutual interest is present, suggest one small,
  low-pressure way to continue contact around the shared context.
- **skill objective:** User can make a specific, optional continuity move
  without pressuring a new acquaintance for commitment.
- **example situation:** After a study-group introduction, the user and a
  classmate discover they are taking the same difficult introductory course.
- **practice scenario type:** Short roleplay: close an interaction by offering
  one optional, context-linked next step.
- **completion check type:** Specificity-and-pressure check: select or revise
  a follow-up that is clear, optional, and grounded in what was discussed.
- **dimension mapping:** Reciprocity, flow.

## 4. Lesson content model

Every v1 lesson uses the same short, active anatomy:

1. **One clear concept** — one behavior, expressed in plain language.
2. **A realistic example** — one office/campus newcomer moment that makes
   the behavior concrete.
3. **Bad, better, and best responses** — contrasting choices with an
   explanation of the conversational effect. These illustrate judgment;
   they are not copy-paste replies or a substitute for the user’s own voice.
4. **A small decision or exercise** — for example, identify a cue, choose a
   proportionate next move, or revise a response.
5. **A short practice scenario** — a bounded roleplay or draft exercise that
   lets the user try the behavior in context.
6. **A completion check** — an understanding check tied to the lesson’s
   objective, not a record that the user tapped through every screen.

**Session design target:** One lesson is designed for a short 5–7 minute
sitting, including practice and the completion check. This is an explicit
working assumption, not validated behavior; see Assumption 3 in §6.

### Unlock rule

In v1, a lesson unlocks the next lesson only after the user demonstrates
understanding in its completion check. This choice keeps the visible path
progressive while honoring the product brief’s requirement that progression
not be earned by tapping through screens. It does **not** decide a passing
threshold, retry experience, or deterministic-versus-model grading; those
are deliberately deferred in §8.

### Spaced repetition

Completed skills reappear as short review prompts inside later lessons:

- Opening and setting cues from L01–L03 resurface in L05 and L08.
- Turn balance, acknowledgement, and follow-ups from L04–L06 resurface in
  L07, L09, and L10.
- Boundaries and warm exits from L08–L11 resurface in L12’s continuity drill.
- A coaching diagnosis may recommend the mapped completed lesson for a
  targeted review; it does not treat completion as permanent mastery.

The v1 product rule is approximate resurfacing two to four lessons after a
skill first appears. It intentionally does not specify a scheduling algorithm,
notification cadence, or streak mechanic.

## 5. Dimension → lesson routing table

The coaching loop diagnoses warmth, curiosity, reciprocity, and flow. The
ordered mappings below let a weak dimension route to a specific existing
lesson, beginning with the earliest relevant incomplete lesson; a completed
lesson remains eligible for review when the diagnosis repeats the same gap.

| Diagnosed dimension | Ordered lesson ids that train it |
| --- | --- |
| Warmth | `l01-first-hello` → `l02-use-the-setting` → `l05-show-you-heard` → `l08-handle-the-pause` → `l11-end-warmly` |
| Curiosity | `l03-easy-first-question` → `l04-answer-and-return` → `l06-follow-the-thread` → `l07-share-and-make-space` → `l09-read-the-room` → `l10-build-on-common-ground` |
| Reciprocity | `l04-answer-and-return` → `l05-show-you-heard` → `l06-follow-the-thread` → `l07-share-and-make-space` → `l10-build-on-common-ground` → `l12-make-continuity-easy` |
| Flow | `l01-first-hello` → `l02-use-the-setting` → `l03-easy-first-question` → `l08-handle-the-pause` → `l09-read-the-room` → `l11-end-warmly` → `l12-make-continuity-easy` |

## 6. Assumptions (explicitly unvalidated)

The interview gate was waived. The following are design positions, not facts
about users, and must not be treated as validated until the listed revisit
trigger produces contrary or supporting evidence.

### Assumption 1 — Primary payer

**Working assumption:** The first paid proposition can serve both students
and early-career professionals, so the same beginner path uses campus and
office examples rather than choosing one payer now.

**Revisit trigger:** Early payment, waitlist, refund, or usage data shows a
material difference in conversion, willingness to pay, or desired scenario
world between students and early-career professionals.

### Assumption 2 — Peak-pain relocation moment

**Working assumption:** The path starts with first-week openings because it
is designed to be useful at arrival, while later lessons also serve the first
month and first social events.

**Revisit trigger:** Early users or future interviews consistently identify a
different moment—such as the first team social or post-orientation period—as
the point of highest need.

### Assumption 3 — Tolerable lesson and session length

**Working assumption:** A 5–7 minute lesson, including one practice drill and
one completion check, is a tolerable short session for this path.

**Revisit trigger:** Completion, abandonment, replay, or qualitative feedback
indicates that users rush, skip practice, or want materially longer sessions.

### Assumption 4 — Practice-drill participation

**Working assumption:** Users will attempt a bounded practice drill when it
is directly connected to a realistic upcoming or recent newcomer situation.

**Revisit trigger:** Practice-start and practice-completion behavior shows
that users regularly skip drills even when they complete lesson material.

### Assumption 5 — Return and streak mechanics

**Working assumption:** The path should support return through useful review
and real-life relevance, not require a streak mechanic or daily attendance
condition to make progress meaningful.

**Revisit trigger:** Early retention behavior shows that optional review is
insufficient, or user feedback identifies a specific, non-pressuring return
cue worth testing. Any streak rule remains a separate product decision.

### Assumption 6 — Office/campus scenario world

**Working assumption:** Combining office and campus moments in one path gives
the relocation wedge enough shared context without fragmenting v1 into
separate tracks.

**Revisit trigger:** Users in either setting report that the other setting’s
examples reduce relevance, or one setting demonstrates substantially stronger
engagement and warrants a focused variant after v1.

### Assumption 7 — Beginner sequencing

**Working assumption:** Opening → sustaining → mutuality → continuity is the
most useful beginner order for this path.

**Revisit trigger:** Completion checks, coaching routes, or real-life
reflections show repeated need for a later skill before its prerequisite or
show that the sequence does not produce observable transfer.

## 7. Non-goals for the v1 path

- No dating track or dating-message curriculum.
- No ESL or non-native-speaker track; language fluency is not a distinct v1
  curriculum problem.
- No advanced, professional-networking, or other expansion tracks.
- No manipulation, coercion, deceptive tactics, or pressure around another
  person’s boundaries.
- No reply-generator framing. Examples teach choices and transferable
  principles; they do not make a final copy-paste reply the end product.
- No attempt to replace therapy, diagnose mental-health conditions, or make
  claims about another person’s private intent.

## 8. Deliberately deferred

This path defines product sequence and routing only. The following remain in
`ARCHITECTURE.md`’s **“Deferred — not yet specified”** area and require their
own design work before implementation:

- Technical lesson-content schema and storage representation.
- Quiz/completion-check grading mechanics, including deterministic versus
  model-graded evaluation and any passing threshold.
- Lesson authoring pipeline, including human authoring, generated drafts, and
  review workflow.
- Streak implementation, persistence, notifications, and exact eligibility
  rule.
- The **“talk with the coach”** surface: its scope, interaction design, and
  relationship to diagnosis or practice.
